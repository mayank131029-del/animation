    Animation Principles

    What are the 12 Principles of Animation?

    What is Animation?

    Animation – a term that instantly conjures images of bright, vibrant worlds and lovable characters. But there’s more to animation than meets the eye. It’s a vast universe with many different styles and disciplines to explore. Today, we’ll explore the various types of animations that exist and the movies and shows we all know and love that were created from them.

    12 Principles of Animation!

    When we think of animation, our mind immediately goes to Disney movies. They have been an integral part of our childhood and hold a special place in the hearts of many of us. Their captivating animations and fairytale stories have made our childhood memorable. Despite being founded a century ago, Disney still manages to charm people of all ages with their timeless creations.

    12 Principles of Animation are:
    • 1. Squash and stretch
    • 2. Anticipation
    • 3. Staging
    • 4. Straight ahead action and pose to pose
    • 5. Follow through and overlapping action
    • 6. Slow in and slow out
    • 7. Arc
    • 8. Secondary action
    • 9. Timing
    • 10. Exaggeration
    • 11. Solid Drawing
    • 12. Appeal

    1. Squash and Stretch

    This principle gives objects a sense of weight, volume, and flexibility by stretching them during fast movement and squashing them during impact, while maintaining the object’s overall form and believability.

    2. Anticipation

    Anticipation prepares the audience for an upcoming action by showing a small preparatory movement, making the main action clearer, more realistic, and easier to understand.

    3. Staging

    Staging focuses on presenting an idea, action, or emotion in the clearest way possible using composition, camera angle, timing, and movement so the audience immediately understands what is happening.

    4. Straight ahead action and pose to pose

    Straight ahead action animates frame by frame in sequence for fluid motion, while pose to pose starts with key poses first, allowing better control, structure, and planning of the animation.

    5. Follow through and overlapping action

    Follow through shows parts of a character continuing to move after the main body stops, while overlapping action means different body parts move at different times, adding realism and natural flow.

    6. Slow in and slow out

    Objects gradually accelerate at the beginning of a movement and decelerate at the end, creating smoother, more natural motion instead of mechanical or sudden movement.

    7. Arc

    Most natural actions move in curved paths called arcs, and using arcs in animation helps create more lifelike, fluid, and visually pleasing movement.

    8. Secondary action

    Secondary actions are additional movements that support and enhance the main action without distracting from it, adding depth, realism, and personality to the character’s performance.

    9. Timing

    Timing refers to the number of frames used for an action, determining the speed, weight, mood, and emotion, and strongly influencing how realistic or expressive the movement feels.

    10. Exaggeration

    Exaggeration pushes actions, poses, or expressions beyond realism to improve clarity, emotion, and entertainment, while still keeping the animation believable and appealing.

    11. Solid Drawing

    Solid drawing emphasizes proper anatomy, weight, balance, perspective, and three-dimensional form, ensuring characters feel realistic, grounded, and consistent in motion.

    12. Appeal

    Appeal means creating characters, poses, and actions that are visually interesting, engaging, and pleasing to watch, helping the audience connect emotionally with the animation.
